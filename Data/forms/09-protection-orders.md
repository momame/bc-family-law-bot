# Protection Orders and Family Violence Forms

## Overview

BC law provides several types of protective orders for people experiencing family violence. These can be obtained through both Provincial Court and Supreme Court. Protection orders and restraining orders are different things with different legal effects.

## Protection Order (Form F54)

A protection order is made under Part 9 of the Family Law Act (FLA). It is specifically designed to protect a family member from family violence.

### What a Protection Order Can Do

A protection order can include any or all of the following conditions:
- No contact: the respondent must not contact or communicate with the protected person
- No-go: the respondent must stay away from specific places (home, workplace, school)
- No weapons: the respondent must surrender any weapons
- Police enforcement: police can arrest the respondent for breaching the order
- Other conditions the court considers necessary for protection

### Who Can Apply for a Protection Order

A protection order can be requested by an at-risk family member. This includes spouses, former spouses, people who have lived together in a marriage-like relationship, parents of a child, and people who have a close personal relationship with the respondent.

### How to Get a Protection Order in Supreme Court

1. File Form F31 (Notice of Application) requesting a protection order
2. Attach Form F30 (Affidavit) describing the family violence
3. In urgent cases, you can apply without giving notice to the other party (ex parte)
4. If the order is granted, it is issued as Form F54 (Protection Order)
5. The order must be served on the respondent
6. File Form SUP916 (Request for Service of Family Protection Order) to have the order entered into the Protection Order Registry

### Protection Order Registry

All protection orders are entered into the Protection Order Registry, which police can access 24/7. This allows police anywhere in BC to verify and enforce the order. You can request a search of the registry using Form SUP914 (Request for Protection Order Registry Search).

## Restraining Order (Form F54.2)

A restraining order is different from a protection order. It is made under section 183 of the FLA and is available as part of a family law case (not as a standalone application).

### Differences Between Protection Orders and Restraining Orders

- Protection orders are specifically for family violence situations
- Restraining orders can address broader conduct restrictions
- Protection orders are entered into the Protection Order Registry; restraining orders may not be
- Breach of a protection order is a criminal offence; breach of a restraining order may require a contempt application
- Protection orders can be obtained in both Provincial Court and Supreme Court
- Restraining orders in the family law context are only available in Supreme Court

## Form F54.1 - Order Terminating a Protection Order

This form is used when a protection order is no longer needed and a party applies to have it terminated. The court must be satisfied that termination is appropriate and safe.

## Emergency and Urgent Protection

In emergency situations, protection orders can be obtained very quickly — sometimes on the same day. If the court is not sitting, an application can be made to a justice of the peace for a temporary order. The temporary order is then reviewed by a judge at the earliest opportunity.

## Provincial Court Protection Orders (Form 10)

In Provincial Court, Form 10 (Application About a Protection Order) is used to apply for a protection order. Provincial Court protection orders have the same legal effect as Supreme Court protection orders and are also entered into the Protection Order Registry.

Provincial Court is often faster for obtaining protection orders because the process is simpler and hearing times are generally shorter.
