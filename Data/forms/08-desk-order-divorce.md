# Desk Order Divorce — The Uncontested Divorce Process

## What is a Desk Order Divorce?

A desk order divorce is the simplest way to get a divorce in BC Supreme Court. It is called "desk order" because a judge reviews the application on paper at their desk — there is no court hearing and no need to appear in court.

A desk order divorce is available when the divorce is uncontested, meaning either the respondent agrees or the respondent has not filed a response.

## Requirements for a Desk Order Divorce

Before you can apply for a desk order divorce, the following must be true:
- You and your spouse have been separated for at least one year (the most common ground for divorce in Canada)
- At least one spouse has been ordinarily resident in BC for at least one year before filing
- If there are children, adequate arrangements have been made for their care
- The respondent has either filed a response agreeing to the divorce, or has not responded at all (default)

## Forms Required for a Desk Order Divorce

### Step 1: Start the Case
- File Form F3 (Notice of Family Claim) or Form F1 (Notice of Joint Family Claim)
- If using Form F3, personally serve the respondent and file proof of service (Form F15 or F16)

### Step 2: Wait for Response Period
- Respondent has 30 days to respond with Form F4
- If no response is filed, you can proceed by default

### Step 3: Financial Disclosure (if applicable)
- If claiming child support or spousal support, file Form F8 (Financial Statement)
- If claiming child support, also file Form F37 (Child Support Affidavit)

### Step 4: File the Desk Order Divorce Package
- Form F35: Requisition — Undefended Family Law Case (tells the registry you want a desk order divorce)
- Form F38: Affidavit — Desk Order Divorce (sworn statement providing all required divorce information)
- Form F36: Certificate of Pleadings (prepared by the registry based on filed documents)
- Draft divorce order using Form F52 (Final Order)

### Step 5: Judge Reviews on Paper
- A judge reviews all documents at their desk
- If everything is in order, the judge signs the divorce order
- If the judge has questions or concerns, they may request corrections or set a hearing

### Step 6: After the Divorce Order
- The divorce takes effect on the date the order is made
- You can legally remarry 31 days after the divorce order (to allow time for appeal)
- Form F56 (Certificate of Divorce) is the official proof of divorce
- Request Form F56 from the registry after the 31-day appeal period

## Form F38 - Affidavit (Desk Order Divorce)

This is a critical form in the desk order process. It is a sworn affidavit that provides:
- Confirmation of the marriage (date, place, certificate details)
- Confirmation of separation (date and circumstances)
- Details about children of the marriage
- Confirmation that arrangements for children are satisfactory
- Details about any agreements or orders already in place
- Information about any other divorce proceedings
- Confirmation of residency in BC

## Form F35 - Requisition (Undefended Family Law Case)

Form F35 is the administrative form that formally requests the registry to process your desk order divorce. It tells the registry that the case is undefended and ready for a judge's review.

## Form F36 - Certificate of Pleadings

Form F36 is prepared by the court registry, not by you. It certifies the current state of all documents filed in the case. The registry prepares this after you file your requisition (Form F35).

## Form F56 - Certificate of Divorce

Form F56 is your official proof that the divorce has been granted. It is available 31 days after the divorce order. You need this document to legally remarry.

## Common Reasons a Desk Order Divorce is Rejected

- Incomplete or missing forms
- Errors in the affidavit (Form F38)
- Child support calculations that do not follow the Federal Child Support Guidelines
- Insufficient information about arrangements for children
- The divorce affidavit contradicts information in other filed documents
